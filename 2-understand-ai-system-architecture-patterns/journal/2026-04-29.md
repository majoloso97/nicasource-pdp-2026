### Journal
Interesting to find out that a 2023 article presents situations and concerns that now are standard practice. The difficult part was never just using a model API, but building a dependable system around it, and keep it well integrated with the rest of the system.

Something I noticed is the idea that prompts behave like configuration, but it's not rare that people oversee the importance to manage them with the discipline of 'traditional' software artifacts. Versioning, testing, ownership, and rollback are equally important. On the erorr, side, a failure of the overall system can't always be atributed to the LLM, or to the orchestration software around it, yet sometimes model failures can seem like system failures. It's important to consider that issues with instructions, context, constraints and expectations must also be approached with a systemic, engineering mindset.

As with any software product, tradeoffs are always present, for example, when picking a model cost and latency are factors to consider. It's relevant that not always the latest model is necessarily the best one for the task at hand, and it's possible that cheaper models perform just as good as the most recent/powerful ones.

Last observation: What is now called agents or workflows under the hood is just newer labels over the same foundational features: chaining steps, using tools, routing decisions, loops. This is not to say the tools are simple, but being able to strip away the unecessary complexity allows to better understand how to build robust systems, with the old KISS (keep it simple) mentality. The model and prompt matter, but evaluation and orchestration matter maybe more than what can be thought initially.
